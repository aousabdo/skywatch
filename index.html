    Methodology & sources

    How Skywatch is built, and what it does and doesn't claim.

    Primary data — FAA UAS Sightings

    Every US point is one drone sighting from the FAA UAS Sightings public records (quarterly, FY2020–present). The records contain only date, state, city, and a narrative — no coordinates. Altitude, incident class, and UAS descriptor are parsed from the narrative text.

    Geocoding & precision

    Each sighting is placed at a centroid via the US Census Gazetteer, with the precision recorded per point and shown in every popup:

    • · city — exact Census place match (~91%)
    • · alias — curated metro/airport centroid (~6%)
    • · state — fallback state centroid (~2%)

    Nothing is placed at a fabricated precise location.

    Proximity alerts — colocation, not incursion

    "Near a base" is the great-circle distance from a sighting's city centroid to the nearest military-installation boundary (HIFLD). Because sightings are city-centroids, proximity is driven heavily by urban bases (Fort Hamilton in NYC, Fort McPherson in Atlanta). The hotspots panel ranks proximity for an operator to judge — it does not assert intent or confirmed incursion.

    Critical infrastructure

    654 military installations (HIFLD "USA Military Bases") and 917 large/medium US airports (OurAirports, public domain).

    International incidents — curated

    The overseas layer is a curated, illustrative set of 30 well-known, publicly reported drone incidents (2017–2024) — Gatwick, Abqaiq, Moscow, Tower 22, the Red Sea, and others — each carrying a source attribution. It is not a comprehensive database; it exists to frame the US picture against notable overseas events, and is labelled "curated" throughout.

    Build

    Static and client-side — no backend, no login, no paid APIs. Source and pipeline: github.com/aousabdo/skywatch.
